# Model Card — News Topic Classifier

## Model Overview

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Model type** | Multinomial Logistic Regression |
| **Task** | Multi-class text classification (topic prediction) |
| **Classes** | Topics discovered by BERTopic — run `python -m src.describe_topics` for current list |
| **Input** | Raw article text (title + content) |
| **Output** | Predicted topic ID + confidence probability |
| **Training date** | March 2026 |
| **Framework** | scikit-learn 1.8.0 |

---

## Intended Use

### Primary use case
Automatically classify English-language news articles into one of 73 topic categories discovered from a corpus of US news (2016–2017). Intended for:
- Content organization and tagging at scale
- Exploratory analysis of large news corpora
- Demonstration of hybrid unsupervised + supervised NLP pipeline

### Out-of-scope uses
- **Real-time breaking news classification** — model was trained on 2016–2017 articles; topics and vocabulary may have drifted
- **Non-English articles** — preprocessing and TF-IDF vocabulary are English-only
- **Short snippets** — articles under ~200 characters may not have enough signal for reliable prediction
- **High-stakes decisions** — this model is a portfolio demonstration, not a production system validated for consequential use

## Training Data

**Dataset:** All The News — [Kaggle](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/snapcrack/all-the-news)

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Total raw articles | 142,570 |
| After cleaning (null/duplicate removal) | 142,036 |
| After length filtering (<200 chars) | 141,112 |
| After BERTopic labeling (outliers removed) | 87,392 |
| Training split (80%) | 69,913 |
| Test split (20%) | 17,479 |
| Date range | 2016–2017 |
| Publications | 15 US news outlets |
| Language | English |

**Publications represented:**
Breitbart, New York Post, NPR, CNN, Washington Post, Reuters, Guardian, New York Times, The Atlantic, Business Insider, National Review, Talking Points Memo, Vox, BuzzFeed News, Fox News.

### Label generation
Labels were **not manually annotated**. They were generated by BERTopic — an unsupervised topic model that clusters articles by semantic similarity using sentence embeddings. The 73 topic labels represent the natural topical structure BERTopic discovered in the corpus.

This means:
- Labels reflect statistical patterns in the data, not a human-defined taxonomy
- Two topics may overlap conceptually (e.g. multiple political topics)
- ~38% of articles were assigned to topic -1 (outlier) and excluded from training

## Topic Labels

Topics are discovered automatically by BERTopic and vary depending on the training run. The authoritative label list is always in `data/processed/topic_info.csv`, generated fresh each time `topic_model.py` runs.

To view the current topic list:

```bash
python -m src.describe_topics
```

To load them programmatically:

```python
import pandas as pd
topic_info = pd.read_csv("data/processed/topic_info.csv")
topic_info = topic_info[topic_info["Topic"] != -1].sort_values("Topic")
print(topic_info[["Topic", "Count", "Name"]])
```

Each topic name follows the format `{id}_{keyword1}_{keyword2}_{keyword3}_{keyword4}` — the keywords are the most representative words in that cluster according to c-TF-IDF.

## How the Model Works

### Feature extraction
1. **Preprocessing:** lowercase, remove URLs/punctuation/stopwords (NLTK)
2. **Vectorization:** TF-IDF with 50,000 features, bigrams, sublinear term frequency
3. **Input:** title concatenated with content body

### Classification
Logistic Regression learns a weight vector for each of the 73 topic classes. At prediction time, it computes a score for each class and returns the highest-scoring class along with a probability vector (via softmax).

### Why Logistic Regression over XGBoost
Both models were trained and compared. LR achieved F1=0.883 vs XGBoost's F1=0.839. For high-dimensional sparse TF-IDF features, the word→topic relationship is approximately linear — LR is purpose-built for this structure. Full comparison logged in MLflow.

## Evaluation Results

Evaluated on a held-out 20% test split (17,479 articles), stratified by topic class.

### Overall metrics

| Metric | Score |
|--------|-------|
| Accuracy | 0.898 |
| F1 (macro) | 0.883 |
| F1 (weighted) | ~0.898 |

### Per-class performance summary

| Performance tier | F1 range | Typical topics |
|-----------------|----------|----------------|
| Excellent | 0.90–0.99 | Sports, international affairs, entertainment (distinct vocabulary) |
| Good | 0.80–0.90 | Crime/police, economics, health |
| Challenging | 0.70–0.80 | Topics with overlapping political vocabulary |

### Common failure modes
The model most often confuses topics that share vocabulary:
- Multiple political topics (Trump, GOP, Senate, elections) cross-contaminate each other
- US domestic politics and foreign policy topics overlap around key names
- Economic topics and business/corporate topics share financial vocabulary

## Explainability

Model predictions are explainable via two methods:

**1. Global importance (model coefficients)**
For each topic, `model.coef_[k]` gives the weight of every word toward that topic. For a linear model, this is mathematically equivalent to the mean absolute SHAP value. Example: for the "EU/Brexit" topic, highest-weight words are `eu`, `brexit`, `britain`, `european`, `british`.

**2. Per-article SHAP explanation**
`shap.LinearExplainer` computes SHAP values on the non-zero features of a specific article. This shows which words in *that article* pushed the prediction toward the predicted topic.

All explanation outputs are saved to `data/processed/reports/`.

## Limitations

### Data limitations
- **Temporal scope:** Articles are from 2016–2017. US political topics are heavily shaped by the 2016 election cycle. Topics like "Clinton email server" or "Brexit referendum" are time-specific.
- **Publication bias:** Dataset overrepresents certain outlets (Breitbart: 23k articles, Fox News: 4k). Topic discovery may reflect outlet-specific editorial emphasis.
- **English only:** No multilingual support.

### Model limitations
- **No semantic understanding:** TF-IDF doesn't understand that "car" and "automobile" are synonyms. Phase 2 (DistilBERT fine-tuning) addresses this.
- **73 fixed topics:** The model can only predict topics it was trained on. A genuinely novel topic (e.g. a new technology that didn't exist in 2016–2017) has no corresponding class.
- **Unsupervised label quality:** Topics were discovered, not designed. Some topic boundaries are arbitrary — the model inherits any ambiguity from BERTopic's clustering.
- **Short article degradation:** Articles under ~500 chars may produce unreliable predictions due to sparse TF-IDF vectors.

### Known biases
- Topics and vocabulary reflect US political discourse during a polarized election cycle
- Publications with more articles (Breitbart, NY Post) may have disproportionate influence on topic definitions
- Topic labels are keyword-based (e.g. "clinton_fbi_emails") and may not be neutral descriptors

---

## Ethical Considerations

- This model should not be used to automatically label or moderate content without human review
- Political topic classification can be sensitive — misclassification has reputational implications
- The training data includes partisan outlets; predictions may reflect outlet-specific framing

## Phase 2 Upgrade Path

Per the project roadmap, Phase 1 (this model) establishes a baseline. Phase 2 replaces TF-IDF + Logistic Regression with a fine-tuned **DistilBERT** classifier:

| | Phase 1 (current) | Phase 2 (planned) |
|-|-------------------|-------------------|
| Features | TF-IDF (50k sparse) | DistilBERT embeddings (768-dim dense) |
| Classifier | Logistic Regression | Fine-tuned transformer |
| Semantic understanding | None (bag-of-words) | Full contextual embeddings |
| Inference speed | ~1ms | ~50ms |
| Hardware | CPU | MPS (Apple Silicon) |

Phase 2 begins after Phase 1 is fully validated, tested, and deployed.
